The *US v. Google* antitrust case is currently in the **appeals phase** following a December 2025 final remedies ruling that imposed behavioral restrictions but rejected a structural breakup of the company [11][20]. In February 2026, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) and a coalition of states filed notices to appeal this decision, seeking more aggressive measures such as the forced sale of the Chrome browser [20][23].

## Current Status of the Case
Following a landmark September 2, 2025, ruling that identified Google as an illegal monopolist in online search, Judge Amit Mehta finalized the case's remedies on December 5, 2025 [4][11]. 
*   **Appeals:** Both parties are currently challenging the outcome. Google is appealing the initial 2024 ruling that it broke the law, while the DOJ and 35 states are appealing the "remedies" phase, arguing the court's requirements did not go far enough to restore competition [8][20][22].
*   **Oversight:** In March 2026, Judge Mehta appointed a five-member **Technical Committee** to monitor Google’s compliance with the court-ordered behavioral remedies [15][21].
*   **Ongoing Litigation:** Separately, Google continues to face other antitrust challenges, including a consumer lawsuit in California regarding search dominance and a DOJ case focused on its advertising technology [16][24].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies
The DOJ initially proposed a suite of aggressive "structural" and "behavioral" remedies intended to dismantle Google's monopoly power:
*   **Forced Divestiture:** The DOJ sought to force Google to sell its **Chrome browser** to prevent it from using the platform to favor its own search engine [20][23].
*   **Ban on Exclusive Contracts:** A core proposal was a total ban on the multi-billion dollar exclusive deals Google signs with companies like Apple and Samsung to remain the default search engine on devices [3][4].
*   **Data Sharing and Transparency:** The DOJ proposed that Google be required to share its search data with competitors and provide more transparency regarding its default search agreements [4][29].
*   **AI Protections:** Proposals included limiting Google’s ability to use its search dominance to gain an unfair advantage in the emerging generative AI market [6].

## Comparison: US Ruling vs. EU Approach
The US court's current stance and the EU’s regulatory framework represent two different philosophies for managing Big Tech dominance:

| Feature | US Judge's Ruling (Mehta) | EU Approach (EC & DMA) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Mechanism** | Behavioral remedies and court-monitored compliance [11][21]. | Massive fines and proactive regulation via the **Digital Markets Act (DMA)** [19][27]. |
| **Structural Breakup** | **Rejected.** The court stopped short of ordering a breakup or the sale of Chrome [4][29]. | Historically floated as an option, but the EU has primarily relied on fines (e.g., €2.42 billion in the Shopping case) [19][28]. |
| **Default Settings** | Bans specific exclusive contracts that ensured Google was the default engine [3]. | Enforces "choice screens" and fairness in search algorithms to promote equal opportunities for rivals [26][27]. |
| **Focus** | Focused on specific illegal practices (exclusive deals) identified in litigation [3][7]. | Focused on "gatekeeper" status, aiming for broad market fairness and media pluralism [1][27]. |

While the US court opted for data-sharing and transparency over a breakup, the EU is increasingly using the DMA to force Google to change its search result layouts and algorithm practices to accommodate competitors in real-time [26][27].
