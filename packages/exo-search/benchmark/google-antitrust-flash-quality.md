As of March 2026, the **US v. Google** search antitrust case is currently in the **appeals phase** following a February 3, 2026, filing by the Department of Justice (DOJ) and a coalition of 38 states [1][14][21]. While U.S. District Judge Amit Mehta finalized a set of behavioral remedies in December 2025—including the establishment of a five-member Technical Committee to oversee Google's compliance—the DOJ is appealing the court's refusal to order structural remedies, specifically the forced divestiture of the Chrome browser [12][15][21]. This judicial approach differs from the European Union’s strategy, which has historically relied on massive multi-billion euro fines and has recently shifted toward proactive, "gatekeeper" regulation under the Digital Markets Act (DMA) [9][18][27].

## Current Status of US v. Google (Search Case)

The legal proceedings against Google have transitioned from a finding of liability to a contested implementation of remedies.

* **Liability and Initial Ruling**: In August 2024, Judge Amit Mehta issued a landmark ruling declaring Google an illegal monopolist in the online search and search text advertising markets [3][30]. The court found that Google had maintained its monopoly through exclusive distribution agreements that made its search engine the default on billions of devices [30].
* **Remedies Trial and Decision**: A remedies trial took place in September 2025, with closing arguments held on November 21, 2025 [4]. On September 2, 2025, the court issued a pivotal decision on the consequences Google would face, though it notably rejected the most aggressive structuralchanges proposed by the government [6][7][24].
* **Finalization of Remedies (December 2025)**: On December 5, 2025, Judge Mehta finalized the specific remedies, adding technical details to the oversight framework [12]. This included the creation of a **Technical Committee** tasked with monitoring Google’s adherence to the court's behavioral mandates for several years [2][15].
* **The 2026 Appeals**: The case entered a new phase of litigation in early 2026. On February 3, 2026, the DOJ and 38 states filed notices of appeal, specifically targeting the court's decision to let Google "dodge" a forced sale of Chrome [21][24]. Google also filed its own notice of appeal against the original monopoly ruling [13][23]. As of March 5, 2026, the court was in the process of hiring the specific members of the Technical Committee while the appellate process moved forward [23].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies vs. Court-Ordered Outcomes

The DOJ sought a comprehensive overhaul of Google’s business structure, but the court ultimately favored behavioral constraints over a full breakup.

### DOJ’s Proposed Structural and Behavioral Remedies

The DOJ’s proposals were designed to dismantle the "flywheel" of data and distribution that sustained Google's monopoly:

* **Divestiture of Chrome**: The DOJ argued that Google used its Chrome browser to unfairly funnel users toward its own search engine. They proposed a forced sale of Chrome to break this vertical integration [21][24].
* **Ending Exclusive Agreements**: A core proposal was the prohibition of multi-billion dollar payments to companies like Apple and Samsung to secure default search placement [30].
* **Data and AI Access**: The DOJ proposed that Google be required to share its search data with rivals to level the playing field [10]. Furthermore, they sought to prevent Google from using its search dominance to gain an unfair advantage in training generative AI models [5].
* **Ad Tech Remedies**: In a related but separate ad tech case, the DOJ proposed the divestiture of Google’s ad exchange (AdX) and the creation of an **escrow fund** to collect a share of net profits from the exchange to mitigate competitive harm [8][25][29].

### Remedies Implemented by the Court

Judge Mehta rejected the "toughest" remedies, such as the Chrome breakup, citing concerns about the rapidly evolving landscape of generative AI and the potential for structural remedies to hinder innovation [5][22][24]. Instead, the court implemented:

* **Behavioral Mandates**: Restrictions on how Google enters into default search agreements.
* **The Technical Committee**: A five-member oversight body established to ensure Google complies with the judgment [2][15]. This committee is intended to assist the court in implementing and enforcing the complex technical requirements of the ruling [2][23].
* **Escrow and Profit Sharing**: In the ad tech context, proposals included collecting shares of net profit to address the unlawful maintenance of monopolies [8].

## Comparison: US Judicial Ruling vs. EU Regulatory Approach

The US and EU have taken fundamentally different paths to addressing Google’s market dominance, reflecting their distinct legal traditions.

### The US Approach: Judicial and Remedial

The US process is rooted in the **Sherman Act**, requiring a high burden of proof to show that a firm not only has a monopoly but has "unlawfully maintained" it through anticompetitive conduct [25][30].

* **Focus on Specific Conduct**: The US court focused on specific contracts (like the Apple deal) and sought remedies that directly addressed those "exclusionary" acts [30].
* **Reluctance to Break Up**: The US judiciary has shown significant hesitation in ordering structural breakups (divestitures), preferring behavioral remedies that allow the company to remain intact while changing its practices [22][24].
* **Oversight via Committee**: The use of a Technical Committee represents a "middle ground" approach—intensive monitoring without dismantling the corporate structure [15][23].

### The EU Approach: Regulatory and Punitive

The European Union has historically been more aggressive, using both traditional competition law and new legislative frameworks.

* **Massive Fines**: The EU has levied record-breaking fines against Google, including **€2.42 billion** for favoring its own shopping services and **€2.95 billion** for abusive practices in the advertising market [16][18][20].
* **The Digital Markets Act (DMA)**: Moving beyond case-by-case litigation, the EU implemented the DMA to proactively regulate "gatekeeper" platforms [26][27]. The DMA enforces fairness in search algorithms and promotes equal opportunities for competitors without waiting for a years-long court battle [27].
* **Stricter Enforcement in 2026**: As of early 2026, the EU is intensifying its challenges to Big Tech, with regulatory moves expected to lead to even tougher enforcement than seen in previous years [28].
* **Impact of US Rulings on EU**: The US finding that Google is a monopolist is expected to complicate Google’s position in Europe, potentially emboldening EU regulators to pursue even more stringent actions under the DMA [9].

## Summary

* **Appeals Status**: The *US v. Google* case is currently being appealed by both the DOJ (seeking a Chrome breakup) and Google (challenging the monopoly ruling) as of February/March 2026 [1][21][23].
* **Oversight Mechanism**: A five-member Technical Committee has been established by the US court to monitor Google’s compliance with behavioral remedies [2][15].
* **Rejected Remedies**: Judge Mehta rejected the DOJ's request to force a sale of Chrome, opting instead for behavioral changes to avoid disrupting the AI market [5][21][24].
* **US vs. EU**: The US approach remains focused on judicial remedies for specific antitrust violations, while the EU has moved toward a proactive regulatory model (DMA) backed by multi-billion euro fines [18][27][28].
