SpaceX’s Starship is a fully reusable, high-capacity launch system designed for low-cost, high-frequency missions, whereas NASA’s Space Launch System (SLS) is an expendable, high-cost rocket optimized for deep-space payload delivery and mission assurance [1][4][6]. In the Artemis program, SLS transports the Orion crew capsule to lunar orbit, while Starship serves as the Human Landing System (HLS) to ferry astronauts to the lunar surface [3].

## Cost and Payload Capacity
The primary economic and technical differences between the two systems lie in reusability and lift capability:

*   **Cost per Launch:** SLS is an expendable rocket, meaning its components are lost after every flight, resulting in a high per-launch cost [1]. In contrast, Starship is designed to land and fly again, which SpaceX intends will substantially lower the cost per launch and the cost-per-kilogram to orbit [1][6].
*   **Payload Capacity:** Starship is designed with a massive payload capacity of over 100 tons (220,000 lbs) to low-Earth orbit (LEO) [7]. Some estimates place its capacity as high as 300,000 lbs [4].
*   **Thrust:** The SLS is a powerful heavy-lift vehicle that generated 34.5 million newtons of thrust at launch, producing more power than 85 Hoover Dams and exceeding the power of the Saturn V [2][10].

## Development Timeline and Artemis Roles
The two rockets follow different development philosophies and serve distinct roles in NASA's lunar exploration strategy:

*   **Development Philosophy:** NASA’s SLS development prioritizes mission assurance and deep-space payload capacity using traditional procurement and design methods [1]. SpaceX utilizes an iterative design process for Starship, focusing on rapid prototyping and testing to achieve full reusability [1][4].
*   **SLS Role in Artemis:** The SLS rocket, paired with the Orion spacecraft, is the primary vehicle for transporting astronauts from Earth to lunar orbit [3]. It is the "backbone" of the Artemis program's deep-space transport [8].
*   **Starship Role in Artemis:** Under the Artemis program, Starship is contracted as the Human Landing System (HLS) [3]. Once the SLS/Orion delivers astronauts to lunar orbit, they will transfer to a modified Starship to descend to the Moon's surface and later return to the Orion capsule [3].
*   **Future Outlook:** While SLS is currently the established heavy-lift vehicle for crewed lunar missions, the continued success of Starship could create political and economic pressure to transition more mission components to SpaceX's lower-cost architecture [6][8].
