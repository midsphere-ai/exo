The **US v. Google** antitrust case is currently in the appeals phase after a federal judge finalized a remedies order on December 5, 2025 [1][23]. While the Department of Justice (DOJ) sought a structural breakup of the company, the court rejected those proposals, opting instead for conduct-based restrictions, such as banning exclusive default search agreements [6][8][30].

## Current Status of the Case
Following U.S. District Judge Amit Mehta’s September 2025 ruling that Google illegally monopolized the search market, the case moved to a remedies phase to determine how to restore competition [11][15][17]. A remedies trial concluded with closing arguments on November 21, 2025, and the final order was issued in early December [12][23]. 

As of early 2026, the case is being contested in appellate courts:
*   **DOJ Appeal**: The Justice Department and a coalition of states are appealing the judge's remedies ruling, arguing it did not go far enough to address the monopoly [1].
*   **Google Appeal**: Google has filed notices to appeal the original liability ruling that labeled it an illegal monopolist [16][24].
*   **Oversight**: The court has mandated the creation of a five-member **Technical Committee** to monitor Google’s compliance with the ordered remedies [9].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies
The DOJ advocated for "sweeping structural remedies" designed to dismantle Google's integrated ecosystem [10]. Key proposals included:
*   **Divestitures**: Forcing Google to sell off **Chrome** and the **Android** operating system to prevent it from using these platforms to favor its own search engine [10][27].
*   **Contract Bans**: Prohibiting the multi-billion dollar exclusive deals that made Google the default search engine on Apple and Samsung devices [6].
*   **Data Sharing**: Requiring Google to share its underlying search data and indexes with competitors to lower the barrier to entry for rival search engines [10].
*   **Ad Tech Escrow**: In the related ad tech litigation, the DOJ proposed an escrow fund to collect a share of net profits from Google’s ad exchange [18].

## Comparison with the EU Approach
The U.S. court's decision represents a different philosophy than the European Union’s regulatory framework:
*   **Structural vs. Behavioral**: While the DOJ pushed for structural breakups (which the U.S. judge ultimately rejected), the EU has primarily relied on massive fines—such as a **€2.42 billion** penalty for search dominance—and behavioral mandates [26][29].
*   **Goal of Law**: U.S. antitrust law is designed to address the monopolization of a market itself, whereas EU competition law focuses on preventing the **abuse of dominance** without necessarily seeking to end the monopoly [21].
*   **AI Considerations**: The U.S. judge specifically rejected some DOJ remedies to avoid hindering Google’s ability to compete in the evolving generative AI landscape, a nuance that differs from the EU's broader digital market regulations [13].
*   **Effectiveness**: Some analysts argue the American framework is better positioned to help competition because it targets specific anticompetitive practices (like exclusive contracts) rather than relying on the broad regulatory "missteps" seen in Europe [3].

As of March 2026, the **US v. Google** search antitrust case is currently in the appeals phase following a landmark September 2, 2025, remedies ruling that imposed conduct-based restrictions but rejected the Department of Justice's (DOJ) request to break up the company [1][4][27]. In February 2026, the DOJ and a coalition of 35 states filed formal notices to appeal this decision, seeking to revive structural remedies such as the forced sale of the Chrome browser [27][29]. While the court established a Technical Committee to oversee Google's compliance with data-sharing and transparency requirements, it stopped short of the more aggressive "breakup" approach favored by regulators and some international counterparts [3][4][22].

## Current Status of US v. Google Antitrust Litigation

The litigation against Google is currently split across two major fronts: the search monopoly case and the digital advertising technology (adtech) case.

### The Search Case (District of Columbia)
The search case reached a critical milestone on August 5, 2024, when U.S. District Judge Amit Mehta ruled that Google had illegally maintained a monopoly in online search and search advertising [21]. Following this liability phase, a remedies trial took place in late 2025 to determine how to restore competition [5].

*   **September 2, 2025 Ruling:** Judge Mehta issued a final judgment that required Google to share certain search data with competitors and increase transparency regarding its default search agreements [4][7]. However, the judge explicitly rejected the DOJ's proposal to force Google to divest its Chrome web browser [22][27].
*   **Appeals Process:** In February 2026, the DOJ and 35 states filed notices of appeal against the September 2025 ruling [27][29]. Simultaneously, Google is appealing the original August 2024 ruling that found it to be an illegal monopolist [9][17].
*   **Implementation:** A Technical Committee has been established to assist the court in implementing and enforcing the conduct remedies, which are set to remain in effect for a specified duration to ensure Google complies with the new transparency and data-sharing mandates [3].

### The Adtech Case (Eastern District of Virginia)
A separate monopolization case regarding Google's advertising technology is also progressing. U.S. District Judge Leonie Brinkema has stated that Google "wilfully engaged in a series of anti-competitive acts" to maintain dominance in the adtech market [12]. 
*   **Current Status:** The DOJ prevailed in this second landmark case, with the court finding Google liable for monopolizing the digital advertising stack [18]. 
*   **Potential Remedies:** Similar to the search case, there is significant speculation regarding whether Judge Brinkema will order a breakup of Google's adtech business, though some experts suggest the outcome may mirror the search case's focus on conduct rather than structural separation [13].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies

The Department of Justice has proposed a suite of aggressive remedies designed to dismantle Google's "moats" and prevent the company from leveraging its search dominance into emerging fields like generative AI [2][6].

### Structural Remedies (The "Breakup")
The most significant DOJ proposal was the **divestiture of the Chrome web browser** [20][27]. The DOJ argued that owning the world's most popular browser allowed Google to self-preference its search engine and collect vast amounts of data that competitors could not access [20]. Despite the DOJ's persistence, the court rejected this proposal in its September 2025 ruling [4][27].

### Conduct and Data Remedies
The DOJ proposed several conduct-based requirements to lower barriers to entry for competitors:
*   **Data Sharing:** Forcing Google to share its search indexes and underlying data with rival search engines to help them improve their results [2][20].
*   **Interoperability:** Requiring Google to make its platforms more compatible with third-party services [20].
*   **Advertising Restrictions:** Implementing new rules on how Google sells search advertising to prevent it from squeezing out competitors [20].
*   **Ad Exchange Escrow:** In the adtech context, the DOJ proposed establishing an escrow fund to collect a share of net profits from Google's ad exchange to mitigate the financial advantages gained through anticompetitive behavior [10].

### Prohibiting Default Agreements
The DOJ sought to ban the multi-billion dollar payments Google makes to companies like Apple and Samsung to be the default search engine on their devices [14][21]. In 2021 alone, these deals were estimated to be worth over $25 billion [14]. The DOJ argued these "pay-for-play" arrangements effectively locked out competition [21].

## Comparison: US Ruling vs. EU Approach

The US court's approach to Google's market dominance differs significantly from the European Union's (EU) strategy, both in terms of legal philosophy and the specific remedies applied.

### US Approach: Conduct over Structure
The US ruling in September 2025 focused on **conduct remedies** and the preservation of innovation in the face of technological shifts [4][6].
*   **AI Considerations:** Judge Mehta's decision to reject a breakup was influenced by the rapidly evolving landscape of generative AI [6]. The court expressed concern that severely limiting Google's ability to compete could stifle AI development [6].
*   **Rejection of Choice Screens:** Unlike the EU, the US court ruled that Google would **not** be required to implement search engine "choice screens" (pop-ups that ask users to pick a preferred search engine) [26][30].
*   **Technical Oversight:** The US has opted for a "Technical Committee" to provide ongoing enforcement, a more hands-on administrative approach than the EU's traditional reliance on periodic audits and fines [3].

### EU Approach: Massive Fines and Regulatory Mandates
The European Commission has historically relied on **record-breaking fines** and strict regulatory frameworks like the Digital Markets Act (DMA) to curb Google's dominance [19][23][25].
*   **Financial Penalties:** The EU has levied multiple multi-billion euro fines against Google, including €2.42 billion for favoring its own shopping services and €2.95 billion for abusive practices in the advertising market [19][23][25].
*   **Abuse of Dominance:** EU rulings, such as the Google Shopping case, focus on "self-preferencing"—the act of a dominant platform unfairly promoting its own services over rivals [24][25]. The EU's highest court recently upheld these findings, rejecting Google's appeals [25].
*   **Structural Threats:** While the EU has primarily used fines, former Competition Commissioner Margrethe Vestager previously floated the idea of a breakup [28]. However, the EU has yet to successfully force a structural divestiture of Google's core business units, making the US DOJ's current appeal for a Chrome sale a potentially more radical step than what has been achieved in Europe [28].

*   **Remedies Implemented:** Current remedies include mandatory search data sharing and increased transparency in default search deals, overseen by a court-appointed Technical Committee [3][4].
*   **Breakup Rejected:** Judge Mehta rejected the DOJ's proposal to force the sale of Chrome, citing the need to maintain Google's ability to compete in the evolving generative AI market [6][22].

While the U.S. has recently allowed limited, case-by-case exports of specific chips like the NVIDIA H200 to China under strict volume caps, China has responded by tripling domestic chip production and implementing aggressive export controls on rare earth minerals [8][14][30]. The European Union is currently prioritizing domestic manufacturing through the European Chips Act while preparing for increased export duties scheduled for June 2026 [11][29].

## U.S. Restrictions on NVIDIA and AMD
The U.S. government is currently drafting and implementing rules that grant it sweeping oversight over the global distribution of AI hardware.

*   **Global Permit Requirement:** Proposed regulations require NVIDIA and AMD to seek U.S. permission for nearly all exports of AI accelerators worldwide, moving beyond previous restrictions that targeted specific "adversary" nations [1][5][17].
*   **Case-by-Case Reviews:** The Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) now reviews license applications for high-end chips, such as the NVIDIA H200 and AMD MI325X, on an individual basis [8][19].
*   **Volume Caps for China:** In a policy shift, the Trump administration has discussed allowing Chinese firms to purchase limited quantities of advanced chips, such as a cap of 75,000 NVIDIA H200 units per firm, rather than maintaining a total ban [18][25].
*   **Regional Expansion:** Export restrictions have been expanded to include various regions beyond China to prevent the diversion of sophisticated AI chips through third-party countries [2][12].

## China’s Strategic Response
China has adopted a dual-track strategy of domestic self-reliance and retaliatory trade measures to counter U.S. pressure.

*   **Domestic Production Surge:** China is undertaking a massive expansion of its domestic semiconductor industry, aiming to triple its AI chip output by the end of 2026 to mitigate the impact of U.S. curbs [14].
*   **Mineral Export Controls:** The Chinese Ministry of Commerce implemented "Announcement No. 61 of 2025," which established the strictest controls to date on the export of rare earth minerals and permanent magnets, essential components for global chip manufacturing [28][30].
*   **Diplomatic and Legal Pushback:** Chinese officials have characterized U.S. export controls as an "abuse" of trade policy and "discriminatory restrictions" [27].

## European Union Status and Policy
The EU is navigating the tech conflict by focusing on internal industrial strength and cautious regulation.

*   **European Chips Act:** The EU is mobilizing €43 billion in policy-driven investment to increase its global market share in chip manufacturing, which stood at only 12.7% in 2023 [7][29].
*   **Export Duties:** EU members are set to increase export duties on certain controlled technologies to 25% starting June 1, 2026 [11].
*   **Strategic List Revisions:** The European Commission recently removed AI and chips from a specific "strategic" tech list in its proposed industrial policy, reflecting a shift in how it categorizes these technologies for trade purposes [7].
*   **Supply Chain Concerns:** The EU remains wary of China's control over raw materials, as the "tech co-dependence" between the U.S., China, and Europe continues to create supply chain vulnerabilities [10][15].

## U.S. Export Controls: Transitioning to a Global Permit Regime

The United States has fundamentally shifted its strategy from containing specific adversaries to exercising "sweeping power" over the global distribution of AI hardware.

### The Global Licensing Proposal (March 2026)
In early March 2026, the U.S. government drafted new regulations that would require NVIDIA, AMD, and other American semiconductor firms to seek federal permits for virtually all exports of AI accelerators worldwide [1][4][23]. This "global licensing" framework represents a departure from previous policies that primarily targeted "countries of concern." Under these proposed rules:
*   **Universal Oversight**: Companies would need explicit U.S. approval to sell advanced AI chips to almost any foreign entity, regardless of location [8][13].
*   **Discretionary Review**: The Department of Commerce would gain the authority to block sales based on national security risks, even in allied nations, to prevent the "leakage" of technology to restricted regions [23].
*   **Impact on NVIDIA**: These rules follow previous restrictions that already forced NVIDIA to halt production of certain high-end models, such as the H200, for the Chinese market [14].

### Expansion Beyond China
The U.S. has already implemented expanded restrictions that cap GPU purchases and restrict exports of sophisticated chips to several nations beyond China [2][17]. This includes:
*   **Regional Caps**: Specific limits on the volume of advanced GPUs that can be purchased by certain countries in the Middle East and other regions perceived as potential transshipment hubs [17].
*   **Unverified End-Users (UVEUs)**: Current policy allows UVEUs to export controlled AI chips without a specific license count, provided that U.S.-headquartered UVEUs maintain at least 50% of their operations or assets under U.S. control [3].
*   **Shift from Bans to Reviews**: For the Chinese market specifically, the policy has evolved from outright bans on specific hardware to a rigorous review process, though many high-performance chips remain effectively blocked [14][15].

## China’s Response: Domestic Acceleration and Retaliation

China has responded to U.S. pressure by treating semiconductor self-sufficiency as a primary national security objective, combining massive industrial subsidies with strategic trade barriers.

### Tripling Domestic Output
To counter the loss of access to NVIDIA’s top-tier hardware, China has undertaken a massive expansion of its domestic manufacturing capacity.
*   **2026 Production Targets**: China aims to triple its current output of domestic AI chips by the end of 2026 [10].
*   **Manufacturing Resilience**: Despite U.S. efforts to shut down advanced-node production, Chinese manufacturers like SMIC have continued to produce logic chips, demonstrating the limits of Western equipment export controls [16][25].
*   **Actor-Based Defense**: China has criticized the U.S. for "discriminatory restrictions" and the "abuse" of export controls, particularly under the returning Trump administration's actor-based approach [11][28].

### Retaliatory Mineral Controls
China has leveraged its dominance in the semiconductor supply chain to implement counter-measures:
*   **Rare-Earth Metals**: Since July 2023, China has maintained strict licensing requirements on the export of gallium and germanium, as well as several compounds essential for semiconductor manufacturing [27].
*   **Strategic Decoupling**: By controlling the raw materials necessary for Western chip production, China seeks to create leverage against further U.S. hardware restrictions [27].

## The European Union’s Position: Strategic Autonomy and Friction

The EU finds itself in a precarious position, attempting to maintain a security alliance with the U.S. while protecting its own burgeoning AI ecosystem from the collateral damage of U.S. export rules.

### Criticism of U.S. Unilateralism
European officials have issued a "strong rebuke" of the latest U.S. export controls [12].
*   **Economic Concerns**: The EU has expressed formal concern that U.S. measures adopted in early 2026 restrict access to AI chips for selected EU entities, potentially hindering the bloc's AI strategy [21][30].
*   **Lagging Market Share**: As of 2023, the EU accounted for only 12.7% of the global chip manufacturing market, making it highly dependent on external hardware for its AI development [6].

### Policy Retrenchment
In a significant shift in industrial policy, the EU recently "gutted" its ambitious plan to challenge China in high-tech sectors.
*   **Removal from Strategic List**: The EU has removed AI, semiconductors, and quantum computing from its "strategic" technology list in proposed industrial policies [6][7]. This move is intended to reduce regulatory burdens on European firms and focus on internal stability rather than aggressive geopolitical competition [7].
*   **Security Measures**: While scaling back its industrial offensive, the EU has simultaneously banned Chinese bodies from participating in critical technology programs related to AI and chips to protect intellectual property [18].
*   **Legal Limitations**: Unlike the U.S., the European Union as a central body does not currently have the legal power to mandate export controls on semiconductor technologies across all member states, though it can coordinate such efforts [22].

## Global Market Implications and Fragmentation

The intensifying "chip war" has led to a fragmented global market characterized by high uncertainty and shifting trade routes.

*   **Economic Uncertainty**: Christine Lagarde, President of the ECB, has highlighted that technology fragmentation is a primary driver of global risk in 2026, complicating monetary policy and industrial planning [29].
*   **Allied Tensions**: The U.S. push for global permits has created friction with democratic allies who fear their own tech sectors will be stifled by Washington’s licensing requirements [12][20].
*   **Negotiation Cooldown**: Some analysts suggest that the U.S. Department of Commerce may slow the introduction of *new* rules later in 2026 to allow for ongoing trade negotiations with China, though the drafted global permit rules remain the current focus [26].

## Summary
*   **U.S. Global Licensing**: The U.S. is drafting rules to require permits for nearly all global exports of NVIDIA and AMD AI chips, moving beyond China-specific bans to a universal oversight model [1][4][23].
*   **China’s 2026 Surge**: China is on track to triple its domestic AI chip production by the end of 2026 to mitigate the impact of U.S. restrictions [10].
*   **EU Policy Shift**: The EU has criticized U.S. export controls as harmful to its own AI ecosystem and has removed semiconductors and AI from its "strategic" industrial list to focus on internal economic stability [6][12][30].
*   **Supply Chain Retaliation**: China continues to restrict exports of essential minerals like gallium and germanium, creating a "tit-for-tat" dynamic in the semiconductor supply chain [27].
