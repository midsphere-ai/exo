The US v. Google antitrust case is currently in the post-judgment phase following a December 2025 ruling that finalized remedies for Google’s illegal search monopoly [1]. While the court has mandated specific conduct-related changes, Google has officially filed a notice to appeal the underlying decision that labeled it a monopolist [5].

## Current Status of the Case
As of March 2026, the case has moved from the liability phase to the implementation of remedies. In December 2025, a federal judge finalized the specific consequences Google must face [1]. These remedies are set to remain in effect for 10 years [9]. To ensure compliance, the court established a **Technical Committee** to assist in implementing and enforcing the judgment [9]. Simultaneously, Google is pursuing an appeal of the original ruling that found its search distribution contracts violated antitrust law [5].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies
The Department of Justice (DOJ) proposed a "structural" approach aimed at dismantling parts of Google's business to restore competition. Key proposals included:
*   **Structural Breakup:** The DOJ sought the divestiture of **AdX** (Google's ad exchange) to separate it from the rest of the company's advertising technology stack [3].
*   **Interoperability:** Requirements for Google’s **DoubleClick for Publishers (DFP)** to be made fully interoperable with rival ad tech platforms [3].
*   **Contractual Bans:** Broad prohibitions on the exclusive search distribution contracts that Google used to maintain its dominance on mobile devices and browsers [2].
*   **Data Sharing:** Proposals to force Google to share search data with competitors to lower the barrier to entry for rival search engines [6].

## Judge’s Ruling and Comparison to Regulatory Approaches
The judge’s final ruling was notably more conservative than the DOJ’s proposal, opting for **conduct-related remedies** rather than the structural "breakup" the government requested [9][10]. 

*   **Rejection of Breakup:** The court explicitly rejected the DOJ’s push to force Google to sell off business units like AdX [9]. The ruling was described as "limited and cabined," focusing on specific business behaviors rather than changing the company's corporate structure [10].
*   **AI Considerations:** A significant factor in the judge's decision to limit remedies was the "evolving landscape of generative AI" [7]. The court expressed concern that overly aggressive remedies could hinder Google’s ability to compete in the rapidly advancing AI sector [7].
*   **Enforcement Mechanism:** Unlike some regulatory frameworks that rely on heavy fines, the US court opted for a 10-year oversight period managed by a Technical Committee to monitor Google's day-to-day compliance with the conduct restrictions [9].

While the provided context does not detail the European Union's specific regulatory actions, the US ruling is characterized by its focus on maintaining the current market structure while curbing specific monopolistic behaviors to protect future innovation in fields like AI [7][10].

### Answer

As of early 2026, the *U.S. v. Google* search antitrust case is in the appeals process, with both Google and the U.S. government challenging the final remedies issued by Judge Amit Mehta in late 2025 [2, 23, 25]. While the Department of Justice (DOJ) proposed structural breakups, including forcing Google to sell its Chrome browser [8], the judge instead ordered behavioral remedies like banning exclusive distribution contracts and mandating data sharing [15, 16]. In contrast to the U.S. focus on distribution agreements, the European Union has utilized massive fines and the Digital Markets Act to penalize Google for favoring its own services in search results [26, 27].

### Current Status of the Case
In August 2024, Judge Amit Mehta ruled that Google violated antitrust laws to maintain an illegal monopoly in online search [29, 30]. Following a remedies trial, the judge finalized the consequences for the company in December 2025 [24]. He ordered Google to share a one-time "snapshot" of its search index data with competitors [15] and barred the company from entering into exclusive contracts relating to the distribution of its search engine [16]. The judge notably avoided ordering a breakup of the company [16, 17].

The case is currently facing appeals from both sides. In February 2026, the DOJ and a coalition of states announced they will appeal the judge's remedies ruling [2, 23]. Google has also appealed the underlying monopoly verdict and requested a pause on the imposed remedies [3, 13, 25]. 

### DOJ's Proposed Remedies
During the remedies phase, the DOJ pushed for aggressive structural changes to dismantle Google's search monopoly [4, 20]. The government's primary proposal was to force Google to sell off its Chrome web browser, with potential additional requirements to divest its Android operating system [8]. Critics argued these proposals were overly broad [9], and the judge ultimately declined to implement these structural breakups in his final decision [16].

### Comparison to the EU's Approach
The U.S. judge's ruling focuses primarily on lowering barriers to entry for competitors by targeting Google's exclusive default agreements and opening access to its backend search data [14, 15, 16]. 

Conversely, the European Union has historically focused on how Google displays its search results and has utilized heavy financial penalties. The European Commission previously levied a record €2.42 billion fine against Google for abusing its market dominance by giving preferential treatment to its own comparison shopping service [26]. Furthermore, under the EU's Digital Markets Act (DMA), regulators are actively targeting Google for breaking antitrust laws by favoring its own Shopping, Hotels, and Flights services over rival comparison providers [27].

### Answer

As of early 2026, the *U.S. v. Google* antitrust case is in the appeals phase, with both Google and the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) challenging a September 2025 remedies ruling that mandated data-sharing but rejected a company breakup. The DOJ had proposed severe structural and behavioral remedies, including forcing Google to divest its Chrome browser and establishing an escrow fund for ad profits. In contrast to the U.S. court's refusal to break up the company, the European Union has taken a more aggressive regulatory stance, levying over €8 billion in fines and keeping the threat of forced divestitures open if Google fails to comply with competition rules.

## Current Status of the U.S. v. Google Antitrust Case

The legal battle, originally filed by the DOJ on October 20, 2020 [15], reached a critical milestone on August 5, 2024, when a U.S. judge ruled that Google violated Section 2 of the Sherman Act [5, 30]. The court determined that Google spent billions of dollars on exclusive agreements to secure default distribution, thereby maintaining an illegal monopoly in the online search market [29, 30]. 

Following the monopoly verdict, U.S. District Judge Amit Mehta issued a landmark remedies decision on September 2, 2025 [6, 11, 12, 13]. The ruling required Google to implement data-sharing measures and maintain transparency in its default search deals [6]. However, the judge explicitly rejected the DOJ's proposals to break up the company [7, 17]. The court finalized the specific details of these remedies on December 5, 2025 [20].

The case is currently gridlocked in the appellate courts:
*   **Google's Appeal:** Google is actively appealing the underlying August 2024 verdict that found the company illegally held a monopoly in online search [13, 21].
*   **DOJ and States' Cross-Appeal:** The DOJ, alongside a coalition of 38 states, filed a notice to cross-appeal the September 2025 remedies decision [3, 10, 19]. The government argues that the court's mandated consequences are insufficient to restore competition.

In addition to the primary DOJ search case, Google's legal challenges continue to expand. On January 22, 2026, a federal judge in California ruled that Google must face a separate consumer antitrust lawsuit accusing the company of illegally using its search dominance [24]. Furthermore, a distinct antitrust case regarding Google AI is scheduled for February 20, 2026 [4].

## Remedies Proposed by the DOJ

During the remedies phase of the trial, the DOJ pursued a highly punitive approach designed to dismantle Google's market power [8]. The government argued that broad structural and behavioral remedies were necessary to prevent further anticompetitive conduct [27]. 

The DOJ's specific proposals included:
*   **Structural Divestitures:** The DOJ sought to force Google to break up its business by divesting unrelated services, most notably the Google Chrome web browser [7, 26]. 
*   **Financial Penalties and Escrow:** To address Google's dominance in the digital ad marketplace, the DOJ proposed establishing an escrow fund that would collect a share of the net profits generated from Google's ad exchange [14].
*   **Contractual Restrictions:** The government proposed frameworks to strictly limit or entirely end Google's ability to use its monopoly profits and exclusive contracts to secure default search distribution on devices and browsers [28].

Ultimately, the U.S. court rejected the DOJ's most intrusive structural demands, allowing Google to keep Chrome and avoiding a forced breakup [7, 17].

## Comparison with the European Union's Approach

The European Union has adopted a significantly more aggressive regulatory framework to dismantle Google's grip on digital advertising and search [1]. While the U.S. legal system has so far relied on behavioral remedies like data-sharing and transparency [6], the EU has utilized massive financial penalties and the persistent threat of structural breakups.

Key differences in the EU's approach include:
*   **Record Financial Penalties:** The European Commission has fined Google over €8 billion to date for abusing its dominant market positions [16]. This includes a €2.42 billion fine for breaching antitrust rules by favoring its own Shopping, Hotels, and Flights comparison services over rivals [22, 23], as well as a separate €2.95 billion fine levied under older competition rules [25].
*   **The Threat of Divestment:** The most stark contrast between the two jurisdictions lies in structural remedies. While the U.S. court definitively rejected the DOJ's proposal to force a breakup [7], the EU's rulings intentionally keep the possibility of forced divestitures open if Google's compliance with competition laws falters [9]. 
*   **Proactive Concessions:** Facing this aggressive stance, Google has been forced to proactively offer remedies to the EU regarding its digital ad marketplace to stave off further regulatory action [1]. 

## Summary
*   **Current Status:** The *U.S. v. Google* case is currently under appeal by both sides; Google is appealing the August 2024 monopoly verdict, while the DOJ and 38 states are appealing the September 2025 remedies ruling for being too lenient.
*   **DOJ Proposals:** The DOJ proposed severe structural and behavioral remedies, including forcing Google to divest its Chrome browser, ending exclusive default contracts, and creating an escrow fund to capture ad exchange profits.
*   **U.S. Court Outcome:** The U.S. judge rejected the DOJ's breakup proposals, instead finalizing behavioral remedies in December 2025 that require data-sharing and transparency in default deals.
*   **EU Comparison:** The EU has taken a harsher stance, fining Google over €8 billion for market abuse and explicitly keeping the threat of forced business divestitures on the table if the company fails to comply with antitrust regulations.
