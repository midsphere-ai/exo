The *US v. Google* search antitrust case is currently in the appeals phase after Judge Amit Mehta finalized the remedies on December 5, 2025 [4][6]. The court opted for behavioral remedies rather than the structural breakups proposed by the Department of Justice (DOJ), establishing a Technical Committee to oversee a 10-year enforcement period [7][9].

## Current Status and Implementation
Following the conclusion of the remedies trial in late 2025, the court issued a final judgment detailing the consequences for Google's illegal search monopoly [4][5]. 
*   **Appeal:** Google filed a notice to appeal the federal court's decision shortly after the remedies were finalized in December 2025 [6].
*   **Oversight:** A Technical Committee has been established to assist the court in implementing and enforcing the judgment [9].
*   **Duration:** The mandated remedies are set to remain in effect for 10 years [9].

## DOJ Proposed Remedies
The Department of Justice sought what Google characterized as "overbroad" remedies that went beyond the specific contracts at issue in the case [1]. 
*   **Structural Relief:** The DOJ and plaintiffs proposed structural remedies, which typically involve the forced divestiture (breakup) of parts of the business [7].
*   **Behavioral Mandates:** The DOJ requested significant behavioral changes to Google's search distribution and "significant remedies" to dismantle its monopolistic hold on online search [3][7].
*   **Scope:** The proposals aimed to address Google's dominance by targeting the contracts that made it the default search engine on various platforms [1].

## Comparison of the Ruling and Regulatory Approach
The U.S. court's final ruling represents a "behavioral" rather than "structural" approach to regulation.
*   **Rejection of Breakups:** Judge Mehta explicitly rejected the DOJ’s requests for structural relief and additional behavioral remedies [7]. 
*   **AI Considerations:** A primary reason for rejecting more aggressive remedies was the "evolving landscape of generative AI" [8]. The court determined that limiting Google’s ability to compete in the AI sector could be counterproductive [8].
*   **Comparison to EU:** The provided context does not contain specific details regarding the European Union's approach to Google's market dominance. However, it notes that the U.S. ruling is considered a favorable outcome for Google because it avoids the most severe structural penalties sought by regulators [2][7].
