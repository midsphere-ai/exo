## Answer

The US v. Google antitrust case is currently in the appeals phase after a federal judge ruled Google is a monopolist and ordered remedies barring exclusive search distribution contracts. The Department of Justice (DOJ) had proposed severe structural remedies, including the forced sale of the Chrome browser and the termination of a $20 billion default search deal with Apple. Compared to the US, the European Union has taken a more aggressive regulatory approach, utilizing the Digital Markets Act (DMA) and issuing multi-billion euro fines alongside proposals to break up Google's ad tech business.

## Current Status and DOJ Remedies
In 2024, Judge Amit Mehta ruled that Google violated Section 2 of the Sherman Antitrust Act by maintaining a monopoly in the general search services market [3][5]. During the remedies phase in September 2025, Judge Mehta backpedaled on some of the DOJ's more severe requests [19]. The DOJ had shifted its strategy to focus heavily on market competition [4], seeking structural breakups such as forcing Google to spin off its Chrome browser and ending its $20 billion default search agreement with Apple [25][27]. 

Ultimately, the court ordered remedies that bar Google from entering or maintaining exclusive contracts for search distribution and prevent the company from applying its traditional monopolistic tactics to the evolving artificial intelligence market [8][15]. Dissatisfied with the scope of these remedies, the DOJ and 35 states appealed the ruling to continue pursuing the Chrome sale and the termination of the Apple deal [27].

## Comparison to the EU Regulatory Approach
While both US and EU regulators agree that Google holds a position of "super dominance" [21], the EU has deployed harsher structural threats and financial penalties [12]. In September 2025, the European Commission ordered Google to cease abusive practices in its publisher ad tech business and levied a €2.95 billion fine against the company for abusing its control over ad buying and selling [13][16]. The Commission has also proposed breaking up Google's display advertising business entirely [20]. 

Furthermore, the EU relies on ex-ante regulation through the Digital Markets Act (DMA) to force compliance regarding third-party search engines and AI [28]. However, despite these aggressive measures, critics argue the DMA has worsened user experience while leaving Google's search monopoly largely intact two years after taking effect [10].
