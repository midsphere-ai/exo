CRISPR overview

CRISPR-Cas9 is a revolutionary genome editing technology that allows scientists to precisely modify DNA sequences in living organisms.

The system works by using a guide RNA (gRNA) that is complementary to the target DNA sequence. This guide RNA directs the Cas9 enzyme — a molecular "scissors" — to the exact location in the genome where a cut should be made [1].

Once the DNA is cut, the cell's natural repair mechanisms kick in. Researchers can exploit this by either disabling a gene (letting the cell repair imperfectly) or inserting new genetic material at the cut site [2] [3].

Key applications include treating genetic diseases like sickle cell anemia [4], developing disease-resistant crops, and advancing our understanding of gene function [1].
